\title{Nonlinear Breit-Rabi amplification of sub-ppm field inhomogeneity in positronium hyperfine spectroscopy}

\author{Ernest Darell Zerme\~no}
\email{0244552@up.edu.mx}
\affiliation{Universidad Panamericana, Guadalajara, M\'exico}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
In their 1975 precision measurement of the positronium ground-state hyperfine structure (HFS), Mills and Bearman characterized the magnetic field gradients across their microwave cavity by nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) probing and reported a sub-ppm spatial offset $\langle\delta B/B_0\rangle = +0.44$\,ppm averaged over the positronium distribution, which they applied as a linear correction to the central field. We show that propagating this same offset through the full nonlinear Breit-Rabi inversion---a step not performed in the original analysis or any subsequent work---produces an additional bias on the extracted HFS interval of $\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS} = -0.22 \pm 0.06$\,MHz, with no free parameters. The underlying mechanism is a sensitivity coefficient $d\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}/dB_\mathrm{res} \approx 4.5 \times 10^5$\,MHz/T near the Breit-Rabi crossing, which converts ppm-level field offsets into MHz-level HFS shifts. The same calculation applied to the Ritter \etal\ (1984) measurement gives $-0.18$ to $-0.30$\,MHz; for the Ishida \etal\ (2014) superconducting solenoid (0.9\,ppm uniformity), $|\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}| < 0.2$\,MHz, consistent with their agreement with QED. The bias preserves the Lorentzian line shape to better than $10^{-4}$ of the peak height and is largely independent of gas pressure, surviving the standard zero-density extrapolation. These two properties explain why this systematic remained unquantified in the published values for four decades.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

Positronium (Ps), the bound state of an electron and a positron, is the lightest purely leptonic atom and provides a uniquely clean system for testing bound-state quantum electrodynamics (QED)~\cite{Adkins2022,Karshenboim2005}. The current theoretical prediction for the ground-state hyperfine structure (HFS) interval, including corrections through $O(m\alpha^6)$ and partial $O(m\alpha^7)$ contributions, is $\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}^\mathrm{QED} = 203\,391.69 \pm 0.41$\,MHz~\cite{Kniehl2000,Melnikov2001,Baker2014}. The two most precise measurements, by Mills \& Bearman~\cite{Mills1975,Mills1983} ($203\,387.5 \pm 1.6$\,MHz) and Ritter \etal~\cite{Ritter1984} ($203\,389.10 \pm 0.74$\,MHz), both used the indirect Zeeman method in gas-filled microwave cavities within conventional electromagnets. Their weighted average of $203\,388.65 \pm 0.67$\,MHz lies $3.9\sigma$ below QED. A more recent measurement by Ishida \etal~\cite{Ishida2014}, using a superconducting solenoid with 0.9\,ppm field uniformity, obtained $203\,394.2 \pm 2.1$\,MHz---consistent with QED but with insufficient precision to resolve the discrepancy.

The role of magnetic field inhomogeneity as a possible systematic in the conventional-magnet measurements has been recognized qualitatively for over a decade. Miyazaki~\cite{Miyazaki2010} identified field non-uniformity and non-thermalization as ``probable common systematic errors.'' Asai \etal~\cite{Asai2012} noted the same concern in designing a direct 203\,GHz measurement. The ETH Zurich group~\cite{Heiss2018} explicitly designed their experiment to be ``free of systematic effects found in earlier experiments, namely the inhomogeneity in static magnetic fields.'' Mills and Bearman themselves measured the gradients across their cavity by NMR and reported a sub-ppm spatial offset, which they applied as a linear correction to the central magnetic field~\cite{Mills1975}. However, the offset was never propagated through the nonlinear Breit-Rabi inversion that converts the resonance field into the extracted HFS interval. We perform that propagation here.

We show in Sec.~\ref{sec:mechanism} that a sensitivity coefficient $d\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}/dB_\mathrm{res} \approx 4.5 \times 10^5$\,MHz/T near the Breit-Rabi crossing amplifies sub-ppm field offsets into MHz-level HFS shifts. Section~\ref{sec:mills} applies this to the Mills measurement using the published gradients and Ps distribution, with no free parameters, obtaining $\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS} = -0.22 \pm 0.06$\,MHz. Sections~\ref{sec:ritter} and~\ref{sec:ishida} apply the same calculation to Ritter (parameter-bounded) and Ishida (negative control). Section~\ref{sec:invisible} identifies two general properties---Lorentzian shape preservation and pressure independence---that explain why this systematic escaped detection for four decades.

\section{Mechanism: Breit-Rabi amplification}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Positronium in a magnetic field}

The ground-state Hamiltonian of positronium in a magnetic field $B$ along $\hat{z}$ is $H = H_\mathrm{HFS} + H_\mathrm{Z}$, where $H_\mathrm{HFS} = (\Delta/4)\,\bm{\sigma}_e \cdot \bm{\sigma}_p$ is the hyperfine interaction and $H_\mathrm{Z} = g_e \mu_B B\,(S_{ez} - S_{pz})/\hbar$ is the Zeeman interaction. Here $g_e = 2.002\,319\,304$ and $\mu_B/h = 13\,996.245$\,MHz/T. The $m = \pm 1$ triplet states are exactly unaffected by the field; only the $m = 0$ subspace is mixed, giving eigenvalues
\begin{align}
  E_{m=\pm 1} &= \Delta/4, \label{eq:Em1} \\
  E_{\pm} &= -\Delta/4 \pm \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{\Delta^2 + 4V^2}, \label{eq:Epm}
\end{align}
where $V = g_e \mu_B B$. In our numerical implementation, we diagonalize the full $4 \times 4$ Hamiltonian matrix, which agrees with Eqs.~(\ref{eq:Em1})--(\ref{eq:Epm}) to better than $10^{-11}$\,MHz. The measured transition frequency is
\begin{equation}
  \nu_\mathrm{trans}(B) = E_+ - E_{m=+1} = \frac{\Delta}{2}\bigl(\sqrt{1 + x^2} - 1\bigr),
  \label{eq:nutrans}
\end{equation}
where $x = 2V/\Delta$. Figure~\ref{fig:zeeman} shows the four eigenvalues as a function of $B$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig1_zeeman_eigenvalues.png}
  \caption{Zeeman eigenvalues of the positronium ground state. The $m = \pm 1$ states (green, orange) are exactly degenerate at $\Delta/4$ for all $B$. The purple arrow indicates the measured transition $E_+ - E_{m=+1}$.}
  \label{fig:zeeman}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The amplification factor}
\label{sec:amplification}

In the indirect Zeeman method, one locates the central field $B_\mathrm{res}$ at which $\nu_\mathrm{trans}(B_\mathrm{res}) = \nu_\mathrm{MW}$ for a fixed microwave frequency $\nu_\mathrm{MW}$, and inverts Eq.~(\ref{eq:nutrans}) to obtain $\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}$. If the magnetic field is non-uniform, each positronium atom samples a local field $B_i = B_0(1 + \delta_i)$. To leading order, the averaged signal peaks at an apparent field shifted by $\delta B_\mathrm{res} \approx -\langle\delta\rangle B_0$ relative to the true resonance field.

The effect on the extracted $\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}$ is obtained by implicit differentiation of Eq.~(\ref{eq:nutrans}) at fixed $\nu_\mathrm{MW}$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}}{dB_\mathrm{res}} = -\frac{\partial\nu_\mathrm{trans}/\partial B}{\partial\nu_\mathrm{trans}/\partial\Delta}.
  \label{eq:amplification}
\end{equation}
At the operating point of the Mills experiment ($B \approx 0.925$\,T, $\nu_\mathrm{MW} = 3253$\,MHz), we obtain
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}}{dB_\mathrm{res}} \approx +4.5 \times 10^5~\mathrm{MHz/T}.
  \label{eq:amp_value}
\end{equation}
A spatially averaged offset of just $+0.5$\,ppm at $B_0 = 0.925$\,T ($\delta B \approx 4.6 \times 10^{-7}$\,T) therefore produces $|\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}| \approx 0.2$\,MHz---comparable to the statistical uncertainty of the original measurements. This amplification factor of order $5 \times 10^5$ between the fractional field offset and the extracted HFS shift is generic to indirect Zeeman measurements near the Breit-Rabi crossing.

\section{Reanalysis of Mills (1975)}
\label{sec:mills}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Experimental configuration}

Mills \& Bearman~\cite{Mills1975} used a thin silver-plated copper cavity (diameter 112.5\,mm, thickness 12.7\,mm) operating in the TM$_{110}$ mode at $\nu_\mathrm{MW} = 3253$\,MHz with $B_0 = 0.925$\,T. NMR probe measurements at the cavity center yielded $\partial^2 B/\partial x^2 = -14$\,ppm/cm$^2$, $\partial^2 B/\partial y^2 = -28$\,ppm/cm$^2$, and $\partial^2 B/\partial z^2 = +44$\,ppm/cm$^2$ ($z$ along the field). These values satisfy $\nabla^2 B_z = -14 - 28 + 44 = +2$\,ppm/cm$^2$, consistent with $\nabla^2 B_z = 0$ in vacuum to $2.3\%$ of the sum of the absolute values. Table~\ref{tab:experiments} summarizes the experimental parameters for all three measurements.

\subsection{Field model and spatial distribution}

In cylindrical symmetry, the azimuthal average gives $\partial^2 B/\partial r^2 = (\partial^2 B/\partial x^2 + \partial^2 B/\partial y^2)/2 = -21$\,ppm/cm$^2$. For the Gaussian transverse distribution measured by Mills ($\sigma_r = 2.44$\,mm), the azimuthal average is exact: the contribution $\langle\tfrac{1}{2}(\alpha_x x^2 + \alpha_y y^2)\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}(\alpha_x + \alpha_y)\sigma^2 = \alpha_r \sigma^2$ is independent of the individual values of $\alpha_x$ and $\alpha_y$ when $\langle x^2\rangle = \langle y^2\rangle = \sigma^2$. The field profile is
\begin{equation}
  B(r,z) = B_0\left[1 + \frac{1}{2}\alpha_r r^2 + \frac{1}{2}\alpha_z z^2\right],
  \label{eq:mills_field}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_r = -0.21$\,m$^{-2}$ and $\alpha_z = +0.44$\,m$^{-2}$. This model has no free parameters---it is fully determined by the published experimental data.

The positronium transverse distribution was measured by slit scans~\cite{Mills1975}: Gaussian with $\sigma_r = 2.44$\,mm. The axial distribution is uniform within the 12.7\,mm cavity thickness, weighted by the detection efficiency function $w(z) = 1 - b|z|$ with $b = 1.4$\,cm$^{-1}$ (Ref.~\cite{Mills1975}, Eq.~4), which represents the position-dependent coincidence detection efficiency for 2$\gamma$ annihilation events. The weighting remains positive throughout the cavity ($b L/2 = 0.89 < 1$).

\subsection{Simulation procedure}

We numerically reproduce the complete experimental procedure:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Sample $N = 5 \times 10^5$ positronium positions $(r_i, z_i)$ from the Gaussian transverse distribution and uniform axial distribution.
  \item For each scan field $B_0$, compute the local field $B_i = (B_0/B_0^\mathrm{nom}) \times B(r_i, z_i)$.
  \item Compute the local transition frequency using a pre-computed cubic interpolation table (20\,000 points).
  \item The signal at each scan point is the detection-weighted Lorentzian average:
  \begin{equation}
    S(B_0) = \frac{\sum_i w(z_i)\, L_i(B_0)}{\sum_i w(z_i)},
    \label{eq:signal}
  \end{equation}
  where $L_i = (\gamma/2)^2/[(\nu_{\mathrm{trans},i} - \nu_\mathrm{MW})^2 + (\gamma/2)^2]$ and $\gamma = 5$\,MHz.
  \item Fit $S(B_0)$ to a Lorentzian in $B$ to extract $B_\mathrm{res}$.
  \item Extract $\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}$ by numerically inverting Eq.~(\ref{eq:nutrans}).
\end{enumerate}

A baseline computed with the identical pipeline using a uniform field ($B = B_0$ everywhere) is subtracted to isolate the inhomogeneity effect from the nonlinear fitting bias ($\approx -0.03$\,MHz):
\begin{equation}
  \delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS} = \Delta_\mathrm{HFS}^\mathrm{extracted} - \Delta_\mathrm{HFS}^\mathrm{uniform\;control}.
  \label{eq:shift}
\end{equation}
This baseline subtraction is robust: the corrected result is stable to $< 0.001$\,MHz for scan windows ranging from $\pm 3\gamma$ to $\pm 16\gamma$ and from 50 to 800 scan points.

\subsection{Result}

Using the published gradients and the measured positronium distribution with detection weighting, we obtain
\begin{equation}
  \delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}^\mathrm{Mills} = -0.22 \pm 0.06~\mathrm{MHz},
  \label{eq:mills_result}
\end{equation}
where the uncertainty includes the Monte Carlo statistical uncertainty ($\pm 0.001$\,MHz from 20 independent random seeds), the quadratic field model approximation ($\pm 0.03$\,MHz, estimated from the difference between our $\langle\delta B/B_0\rangle = +0.52$\,ppm and the $+0.44$\,ppm reported by Mills \& Bearman using discrete NMR measurements), the uncertainty in the detection weight parameter $b$ ($\pm 0.05$\,MHz for a $\pm 10\%$ variation), and the weak pressure dependence of the radial distribution ($\pm 0.02$\,MHz). The mean field offset over the positronium distribution, $\langle\delta B/B_0\rangle = +0.52$\,ppm, is consistent with the $+0.44$\,ppm computed by Mills \& Bearman; the residual difference reflects the approximation of the true field by a second-order expansion.

The result scales linearly with the gradient magnitude to better than $0.1\%$ (tested up to $4\times$ the measured gradients), confirming that the first-order mechanism described in Sec.~\ref{sec:amplification} dominates. The analytical estimate from the amplification factor, $\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS} \approx (d\Delta/dB_\mathrm{res}) \times \langle\delta B\rangle \approx 4.5 \times 10^5 \times (-0.52 \times 10^{-6} \times 0.925) = -0.22$\,MHz, agrees with the full numerical result to $1\%$.

Figure~\ref{fig:resonance} shows the simulated resonance curve.

\section{Reanalysis of Ritter (1984)}
\label{sec:ritter}
%% ====================================================================

Ritter \etal~\cite{Ritter1984} used a Varian V4012A electromagnet (pole diameter 304.8\,mm, gap 66.7\,mm)~\cite{Theriot1970} with careful shimming. The field is parameterized as
\begin{equation}
  B(r,z) = B_0\left[1 + c_2\,\frac{2z^2 - r^2}{R_\mathrm{ref}^2}\right],
  \label{eq:ritter_field}
\end{equation}
with $R_\mathrm{ref} = 33.35$\,mm (half the pole gap). Ritter \etal\ reported a weighted standard deviation of 0.5\,ppm after shimming, corresponding to $c_2 \approx 3$--$5$\,ppm. We use the actual cavity dimensions ($R_\mathrm{cav} = 76.8$\,mm, $L_\mathrm{cav} = 38.1$\,mm) and positronium confinement region ($R \approx 12.7$\,mm, uniform). \section{Verification with Ishida (2014)}
\label{sec:ishida}
%% ====================================================================

The Ishida experiment~\cite{Ishida2014} used a superconducting solenoid with 0.9\,ppm root-mean-square (RMS) residual non-uniformity in a $40 \times 100$\,mm region, operating at $B_0 = 0.866$\,T and $\nu_\mathrm{MW} = 2856.6$\,MHz. Our simulation gives $\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS} = +0.18$\,MHz for uniform Ps and $-0.05$\,MHz for non-thermalized Ps ($\lambda = 30$\,mm). Both values are well below the experimental uncertainty of $\pm 2.1$\,MHz. The superconducting magnet's high uniformity effectively eliminates this systematic, naturally explaining why the Ishida result agrees with QED.

\section{Two properties that hid the systematic}
\label{sec:invisible}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Lorentzian shape preservation}
\label{sec:shape}

The averaged resonance curve $S(B_0)$ [Eq.~(\ref{eq:signal})] retains its Lorentzian shape to remarkable precision. For the Mills configuration, the maximum absolute residual between the inhomogeneous signal and the Lorentzian fit is $9.6 \times 10^{-5}$ of the peak height, with an RMS residual of $5.3 \times 10^{-5}$. This is indistinguishable from the numerical noise of the uniform-field baseline ($9.3 \times 10^{-5}$ and $5.3 \times 10^{-5}$, respectively).

No diagnostic based on the chi-squared of the fit, the presence of asymmetric shoulders, or any other line-shape analysis could have detected this systematic at the precision of the 1975--1984 experiments.

\subsection{Pressure independence}
\label{sec:pressure}

The bias can be decomposed into a radial contribution from $\alpha_r$ and an axial contribution from $\alpha_z$:
\begin{equation}
  \langle\delta B/B_0\rangle = \underbrace{\alpha_r \sigma_r^2}_{\text{radial: }-1.25\,\text{ppm}} + \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\alpha_z \langle z^2\rangle_w}_{\text{axial: }+1.77\,\text{ppm}}.
  \label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
The axial term, which contributes 77\% of the total offset, depends only on the detection geometry and cavity thickness---not on the gas pressure. Only the radial term depends on $\sigma_r$, which varies weakly with pressure through the positron stopping range. Over the full range of plausible $\sigma_r$ values (1.5 to 5.0\,mm), $\delta\Delta_\mathrm{HFS}$ varies by $\sim 0.1$\,MHz. At Mills's operating conditions, a realistic $\pm 20\%$ variation in $\sigma_r$ changes $\delta\Delta$ by $\lesssim 0.02$\,MHz.

Since the standard extrapolation to zero gas density removes only pressure-dependent systematics, the field-inhomogeneity bias survives this extrapolation as a nearly constant offset.

\subsection{Generality}

Together, these two properties---shape preservation and pressure independence---characterize a class of systematics that escape both line-shape diagnostics and pressure-extrapolation controls. Any inhomogeneity-induced shift that is linear in the mean spatial offset of the field and does not couple to gas-density-dependent dynamics falls into this class. The amplification by the nonlinear Breit-Rabi inversion, which is generic to indirect Zeeman methods, converts what appears to be a ``sub-ppm'' effect into a MHz-level bias on the extracted splitting.

\section{Limitations and the joint thermalization problem}
\label{sec:limitations}
%% ====================================================================

Our calculation isolates a single systematic---the field-inhomogeneity bias propagated through the Breit-Rabi inversion---under the simplifying assumption of a thermalized positronium spatial distribution. In reality, the same atoms that sample the non-uniform field are also subject to incomplete thermalization, which Ishida \etal~\cite{Ishida2014} estimated as a separate $\sim 2$\,MHz systematic in their own apparatus.

These two corrections are not independent. The non-thermalized positronium population samples the magnetic field with a different spatial weight than the thermalized population, and the velocity distribution that drives the gas-density shift depends on the local field through the position-dependent formation dynamics. A correct joint correction requires a self-consistent simulation of thermalization in a non-uniform field, which is beyond the scope of the present work.

We do not combine our field-inhomogeneity result with the published Ishida thermalization estimate to produce a single corrected significance for the historical discrepancy. Such a combination would require: (i) re-deriving the thermalization correction under the gas conditions of Mills (isobutane) and Ritter (N$_2$) rather than transferring Ishida's number, (ii) computing the joint distribution of position and velocity, and (iii) propagating the correlated uncertainty.

We note several additional approximations. (i) The microwave cavity mode (TM$_{110}$) has a spatially varying field amplitude; the position-dependent transition rate $\propto |B_\mathrm{MW}(\mathbf{r})|^2$ is not included in our weighting. We estimate this contributes an additional systematic uncertainty of order $\pm 0.05$\,MHz. (ii) The non-Hermitian corrections from singlet annihilation, which Mills~\cite{Mills1983} showed produce a line-center shift of $\sim 0.5$\,MHz, are expected to cancel to leading order in our baseline subtraction, but we have not verified this numerically; this constitutes a further unquantified uncertainty. (iii) The quadratic field model approximates the true field, as evidenced by the difference between our $\langle\delta B/B_0\rangle = +0.52$\,ppm and the $+0.44$\,ppm computed by Mills from discrete NMR measurements.

The claims of this work are made at three distinct levels of robustness:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Parameter-free.} The field-inhomogeneity contribution to the Mills HFS value is $-0.22 \pm 0.06$\,MHz using only the published gradients and detection weighting.
  \item \textbf{Parameter-bounded.} For the Ritter measurement, the contribution lies in the range $-0.18$ to $-0.30$\,MHz, bounded by the reported post-shimming uniformity.
  \item \textbf{Mechanism-level.} The Lorentzian shape preservation and pressure independence are general properties of the Breit-Rabi amplification mechanism, not specific to any one experiment.
\end{enumerate}

%% ====================================================================
\section{Implications for current and future measurements}
\label{sec:future}
%% ====================================================================

Our results imply that any future precision measurement of the ground-state positronium HFS using the indirect Zeeman method must verify the magnetic field uniformity at the $\sim 1$\,ppm level and demonstrate complete Ps thermalization. The importance of cavity-related systematics has been underscored by recent work at UCL: Akopyan \etal~\cite{Akopyan2021} showed through numerical simulations that microwave reflections produce frequency-dependent power distributions that shift extracted frequencies by several MHz, and Sheldon \etal~\cite{Sheldon2023} confirmed this by redesigning the microwave environment, reducing a $4.5\sigma$ fine-structure discrepancy~\cite{Gurung2020} to $1.3\sigma$. The ongoing programs at ETH Zurich~\cite{ThorpeWoods2026} and the Ramsey spectroscopy techniques at UCL~\cite{Newson2026} introduce new sensitivity to field gradients between interaction regions, making field characterization even more critical.

The self-consistent calculation combining field inhomogeneity with positronium thermalization dynamics---accounting for the correlated sampling of non-uniform fields by non-thermalized positronium---is the key remaining step toward determining the joint correction and its impact on the historical discrepancy.

%% ====================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[(i)] The Breit-Rabi inversion of an indirect Zeeman HFS measurement amplifies fractional field offsets by a factor $\sim 5 \times 10^5$ near the operating point of the Mills, Ritter, and Ishida experiments.

  \item[(ii)] Propagating the field offset published by Mills and Bearman through this amplification, with no free parameters, gives a contribution of $-0.22 \pm 0.06$\,MHz to the Mills HFS value. The same calculation gives $-0.18$ to $-0.30$\,MHz for the Ritter measurement and $< 0.2$\,MHz for the Ishida superconducting magnet, qualitatively consistent with the observed pattern of agreement with QED.

  \item[(iii)] The bias preserves the Lorentzian fit shape to better than $10^{-4}$ of the peak height and is largely independent of gas pressure. These two properties identify a class of systematics that escape both standard line-shape diagnostics and zero-density extrapolation controls used in indirect Zeeman measurements.

  \item[(iv)] The present calculation accounts for $\sim 5\%$ of the Mills discrepancy and $\sim 7$--$12\%$ of the Ritter discrepancy with QED. A self-consistent simulation combining field inhomogeneity with positronium thermalization dynamics is required to determine the joint correction.
\end{enumerate}
